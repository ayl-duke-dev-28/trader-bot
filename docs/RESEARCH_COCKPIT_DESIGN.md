## Problem Statement

The current trader-bot is a paper-first Alpaca trading bot with classical signals, an ML direction model, politician-trade overlays, risk controls, backtesting, and a static NYSE ticker universe. The goal is to evolve it from a 50-name paper bot into a resume-grade quant/trading internship project: a bot that can scan 500+ stocks, find overlooked opportunities before they move, make better paper-trading decisions where possible, and explain why each trade was made.

The project should be useful for personal paper trading, but its second job is portfolio signaling: it should demonstrate research discipline, engineering taste, risk awareness, and clear communication to quant/trading interviewers.

## What Makes This Cool

The cool version is not simply a bot with more tickers. The cool version is a small quant research cockpit:

- It scans a broad 500+ stock universe and produces a ranked daily opportunity list.
- It converts raw signals into auditable trade theses.
- It shows live paper-trading positions in an Alpaca-style dashboard.
- It records why every order happened, including signal components, model confidence, risk sizing, and what would falsify the thesis.
- It turns the project into a strong README artifact with screenshots, architecture, metrics, and trade examples.

The "whoa" moment is an interviewer opening the repo and seeing a system that behaves like a mini research platform: scanner, ranking, execution, risk, dashboard, backtest evidence, and explanation trail.

## Constraints

- This is for personal use and internship applications, not a customer-facing trading product.
- The bot should stay paper-first; live trading is not the design target for this phase.
- The current data layer uses yfinance, which is unofficial and can rate-limit, so 500+ stocks require a smarter scan/cache plan than the current sequential per-symbol fetch loop.
- The current config caps the universe at 50 symbols even though `src/data/nyse_universe.txt` has 94 lines, including duplicates.
- The existing risk controls should remain visible: max position percent, gross exposure cap, max positions, daily loss kill switch, and stop losses.
- The system must be explainable enough for a README and interview discussion. "The model said buy" is not acceptable.

## Premises

1. The 500-stock expansion needs a ranked scanner, not just a larger `max_symbols` value.
2. Every executed paper trade should produce a durable thesis record with signal components, confidence, risk sizing, and an expected failure condition.
3. The dashboard is a resume artifact as much as a trading tool: it needs screenshots, metrics, architecture, and examples that a recruiter or interviewer can understand in under two minutes.
4. Backtest and risk evidence matter more than raw prediction claims. A credible quant project shows returns, drawdown, exposure, trade count, hit rate, and examples of losing trades.
5. The first milestone should avoid a full rewrite. It should reuse the existing modules and add structured outputs around them.

## Search Notes

The external pattern is clear: credible quant platforms center strategy research, backtesting, risk metrics, and transaction analysis rather than bare predictions. QuantConnect/LEAN is a useful north star because it combines algorithm design, backtesting, and multi-asset research. Backtrader's analyzer catalog is a useful metric checklist: drawdown, returns, Sharpe ratio, position value, trade analysis, and transactions are the kind of evidence the README should expose.

A useful research warning: prediction accuracy alone is not enough. A portfolio system must size up when it is right and reduce damage when it is wrong. That means this project should separate prediction, ranking, risk sizing, execution, and evaluation.

## Approaches Considered

### Approach A: Dashboard Over Current Bot

Summary: Add a lightweight dashboard on top of the existing bot. Show current Alpaca paper positions, recent orders, account P/L, current risk caps, and generated explanations for any trades made by the existing loop.

Effort: S
Risk: Low

Pros:

- Fastest route to screenshots and a visible demo.
- Reuses the current `src/trader.py`, `src/risk/manager.py`, and Alpaca broker wrapper.
- Keeps the first change small and easy to explain.

Cons:

- The 500-stock story remains shallow.
- It risks looking like a UI wrapper rather than a quant research project.
- It does not solve the yfinance scaling issue.

Reuses:

- `src/trader.py` for trade cycle behavior.
- `src/broker/alpaca_client.py` for account/position/order data.
- `src/risk/manager.py` for risk state.

### Approach B: Research Cockpit

Summary: Build a daily scanner that ranks 500+ stocks, stores signal components, generates trade thesis logs, feeds the paper trader, and powers an Alpaca-style dashboard. The README becomes a product-quality demo: screenshots, architecture diagram, backtest metrics, and example theses.

Effort: L
Risk: Medium

Pros:

- Strongest resume artifact: demonstrates data engineering, modeling, risk, UI, and communication.
- Gives the bot a real research workflow: scan, rank, decide, execute, explain, evaluate.
- Creates durable artifacts that can be discussed in interviews: thesis logs, metrics, ranked outputs, architecture.

Cons:

- Bigger build than a simple dashboard.
- Requires careful caching and batching so 500+ stocks do not break data fetching.
- Needs discipline to keep the first milestone focused.

Reuses:

- `src/data/universe.py` as the universe entry point.
- `src/data/market_data.py` as the cache foundation, with batching/freshness improvements.
- `src/signals/classical.py`, `src/signals/ml.py`, and `src/politicians/tracker.py` as signal components.
- `src/risk/manager.py` for sizing and safety.
- `src/backtest/engine.py` for benchmark metrics and README proof.

### Approach C: Quant Replay Lab

Summary: Focus first on historical replay and model comparison. For each historical day, show what the bot would have ranked, bought, avoided, and why. Build the live dashboard later.

Effort: M
Risk: Medium

Pros:

- Very strong for quant interviews because it shows evaluation discipline.
- Avoids the temptation to over-optimize live paper trading before proving the research loop.
- Makes model comparison and backtest replay central from day one.

Cons:

- Less exciting visually than a live dashboard.
- Does not immediately satisfy the Alpaca-style operator console goal.
- Paper-trading screenshots come later.

Reuses:

- `src/backtest/engine.py` as the core historical evaluation path.
- Existing signals as replayable rank features.
- Existing universe/data modules after expanding cache behavior.

## Recommended Approach

Choose Approach B: Research Cockpit.

This best matches the goal: personal paper-trading tool plus quant/trading internship artifact. The dashboard gives the project visual polish, but the scanner, thesis log, backtest metrics, and README proof make it credible. The design should avoid claiming real profitability. Instead, it should show a disciplined process for finding candidates, managing risk, and learning from outcomes.

## Recommended System Shape

### 1. Universe Layer

Goal: support 500+ names without turning yfinance into the bottleneck.

Design:

- Add a reproducible universe source, such as S&P 500 plus liquid NYSE/Nasdaq large caps.
- Store canonical tickers in a clean file or generated cache.
- Add metadata: sector, industry, market cap bucket if available, and liquidity proxy.
- Deduplicate and validate symbols before scanning.
- Keep `max_symbols` as a safety cap, but raise it intentionally only after caching works.

First milestone:

- Clean the current universe file.
- Add `src/data/universe_500.txt` or a generated `data_cache/universe.parquet`.
- Configure `max_symbols: 500` only for scanner jobs, not blindly for every live loop.

### 2. Data Cache / Scanner Layer

Goal: rank many stocks daily without redownloading all history every cycle.

Design:

- Separate "daily scanner" from "live paper-trading loop."
- Scanner runs once before market open or after close.
- It fetches/refreshes historical data, computes features, and writes a ranked table.
- Trading loop reads the latest ranked candidates rather than scanning all 500 every hour.

Artifacts:

- `data_cache/rankings/YYYY-MM-DD.parquet`
- `data_cache/features/YYYY-MM-DD.parquet`
- `logs/scanner.log`

Ranking fields:

- symbol
- sector
- composite_score
- classical_score
- ml_score
- politician_score
- model_probability
- liquidity_score
- volatility_score
- risk_adjusted_score
- rank
- reason_summary

### 3. Trade Thesis Log

Goal: every order has an explanation that survives after the trade.

Design:

- Before placing an order, build a thesis object.
- Save it even in dry-run/paper mode.
- Link each thesis to the ranking snapshot and current risk state.

Example schema:

```json
{
  "timestamp": "2026-06-22T09:45:00-04:00",
  "symbol": "ABC",
  "side": "buy",
  "rank": 7,
  "target_dollars": 1250,
  "signal_summary": "Momentum positive, RSI not overbought, ML probability 0.61, politician overlay neutral.",
  "components": {
    "classical": 0.42,
    "ml": 0.75,
    "politicians": 0.0,
    "composite": 0.55
  },
  "risk": {
    "max_position_pct": 0.05,
    "gross_exposure_after_trade": 0.42,
    "stop_loss_pct": 0.05
  },
  "failure_condition": "Exit or reduce if signal turns negative, stop-loss hits, or rank falls out of top 100.",
  "source_snapshot": "data_cache/rankings/2026-06-22.parquet"
}
```

README use:

- Show one winning thesis and one losing thesis.
- Explain what the system learned from each.

### 4. Dashboard Layer

Goal: Alpaca-style operator console, not a flashy landing page.

Suggested first screen:

- Top bar: account equity, buying power, daily P/L, gross exposure, market status, last scan time.
- Left nav: Overview, Rankings, Positions, Thesis Log, Backtests, Risk.
- Main overview:
  - current positions table with symbol, sector, market value, unrealized P/L, thesis link, stop-loss status.
  - top ranked opportunities table with rank, score, reason, model probability, volatility, existing position flag.
  - risk panel with exposure, max positions, daily loss threshold, sector concentration.
  - recent thesis feed with buy/sell decisions and dry-run markers.
- Detail drawer for each symbol:
  - score breakdown
  - recent price chart
  - model probability
  - feature importance / signal contribution
  - latest thesis
  - backtest snippet for this signal family

Dashboard quality bar:

- It should look like something a trader could scan in 30 seconds.
- It should be screenshot-ready for README.
- It should expose uncertainty, not hide it.

### 5. Backtest / Metrics Layer

Goal: prove the system is evaluated, not merely run.

Metrics for README:

- total return
- benchmark return
- max drawdown
- Sharpe ratio or volatility-adjusted return
- win rate
- average win/loss
- trade count
- turnover
- gross exposure
- sector concentration
- top 10 trades by P/L
- worst 10 trades by P/L

Comparison table:

- 50-name universe vs 500-name universe
- classical-only vs ML-only vs composite
- with vs without politician overlay
- equal-weight candidate selection vs risk-adjusted ranking

Important: do not frame this as proof of future profitability. Frame it as a reproducible research process.

### 6. Feature Importance / Explanation Layer

Goal: explain decisions in a way that is honest and inspectable.

Design:

- For classical signals, expose direct component contributions: momentum, mean reversion, MA cross, RSI, MACD.
- For ML signal, expose probability and top engineered features for that prediction if the model supports it.
- For politician overlay, show the relevant disclosure count/amount/lookback when it contributes.
- If feature importance is unavailable for a model, show model inputs and confidence instead of inventing causal explanations.

Good explanation:

"Bought ABC because it ranked 7/500 today. Composite score 0.55 came from positive 60-day momentum, MACD crossover, ML probability 0.61, and neutral politician overlay. Position sized to 3.2% of equity because gross exposure after trade remains under 80%. Failure condition: rank drops below 100 or stop-loss hits."

Bad explanation:

"AI predicts ABC will go up."

## Open Questions

- Should the 500-stock universe be S&P 500 only, all liquid U.S. large caps, or a custom NYSE/Nasdaq mix?
- Should the dashboard be a simple static/local web app first, or a FastAPI-backed live dashboard?
- Should feature importance use XGBoost built-in feature importance, SHAP, or a simpler contribution summary first?
- Should the scanner run pre-market, after close, or manually during development?
- Should the project keep yfinance for the resume demo, or add an Alpaca market-data path later?

## Success Criteria

The project is successful for internship applications if the README can show:

1. A screenshot of a live paper dashboard with positions, rankings, thesis feed, and risk panel.
2. A 500-stock ranking snapshot with score breakdowns.
3. At least 10 structured trade theses from paper or dry-run mode.
4. A backtest comparison of 50-name vs 500-name universe.
5. A metrics table with return, benchmark, drawdown, Sharpe/volatility-adjusted return, trade count, and win rate.
6. A system architecture diagram.
7. A clear disclaimer that this is research/paper trading, not investment advice.
